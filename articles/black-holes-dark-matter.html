Black Holes Surrounded by a Dark Matter Halo

March 2024 · T. Angelov, R. Bekir, G. Gyulchev, P. Nedkova, S. Yazadjiev

The Einstein Cluster Construction

We consider a spacetime geometry describing a superposition of a black hole and a dark matter halo at large distance. The solution is inspired by the Einstein cluster — a stationary self-gravitating system of many massive particles moving on circular geodesics in the field created by their collective superposition, first described by Einstein and subsequently revisited in the literature. Such a system is spherically symmetric, and the metric takes the general static form

$$ ds^2 = -e^{\nu}\,dt^2 + e^{\lambda}\,dr^2 + r^2\!\left(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\!\theta\; d\phi^2\right), $$

where the metric functions $\nu$ and $\lambda$ depend only on the radial coordinate $r$. For $n$ particles of mass $m_p$, four-velocity $U^\mu$, and four-momentum $P^\mu = m_p U^\mu$ satisfying the geodesic equations, the averaged stress-energy tensor over all geodesic trajectories passing through a given point is

$$ \langle T^{\mu}{}_{\nu}\rangle = \frac{n}{m_p}\langle P^{\mu}P_{\nu}\rangle. $$

Evaluating this expression using the symmetries of the system, the only non-vanishing components are the energy density $\rho$ and the tangential pressure $p$:

$$ \langle T^t{}_t \rangle = -\rho, \qquad \langle T^\theta{}_\theta \rangle = \langle T^\phi{}_\phi \rangle = p. $$

The corresponding Einstein equations then read

$$ \frac{1}{r^2}\bigl[r(1-e^{-\lambda})\bigr]' = 8\pi\rho, $$ $$ \nu' = \frac{1}{r}\!\left(e^{-\lambda}-1\right), $$ $$ \frac{e^{-\lambda}}{2}\!\left[\nu''+\frac{\nu'^{\,2}}{2} +\frac{\nu'-\lambda'}{r}-\frac{\nu'\lambda'}{2}\right] = 8\pi p, $$

and imply the following algebraic relation between the energy density and the tangential pressure:

$$ p = \frac{\rho}{4}\!\left(e^{\lambda}-1\right) = \frac{r\nu'}{4}\,\rho. $$

Gravitational Mass and the Metric Functions

Introducing the gravitational mass $m(r)$ enclosed within a sphere of radius $r$,

$$ m(r) = 4\pi\int_0^r \rho\, r^2\,dr, $$

all characteristics of the system can be expressed in terms of this single function. The Einstein equations yield its relation to the metric potential $\nu$,

$$ \frac{r}{2}\,\nu' = \frac{m(r)}{r - 2m(r)}, $$

from which the metric function $\lambda$ and the tangential pressure follow as

$$ e^{\lambda} = \left(1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}\right)^{-1}, \qquad p = \frac{m(r)}{2\!\left(r - 2m(r)\right)}\,\rho. $$

Because of their anisotropic energy-momentum tensor, Einstein clusters are natural candidates for modelling galactic dark matter halos: any observed rotation curve can be reproduced by specifying an appropriate tangential pressure profile, or equivalently by prescribing the mass function $m(r)$. Clusters with King-type, Burkert-type, and Navarro–Frenk–White density distributions have all been constructed in this framework.

The Black Hole–Dark Matter Halo Mass Function

To describe simultaneously a central black hole and an extended dark matter halo, we adopt the following mass function:

$$ m(r) = M_{\rm BH} + \frac{M r^2}{\left(a_0 + r\right)^2} \left(1 - \frac{2M_{\rm BH}}{r}\right)^{\!2}, $$

where $M$, $a_0$, and $M_{\rm BH}$ are constants representing the halo mass, its characteristic length scale, and the black hole mass, respectively. Integrating the Einstein equations yields the metric function

$$ e^{\lambda} = \left(1 - \frac{2M_{\rm BH}}{r}\right)e^{\Upsilon}, $$

with the auxiliary function

$$ \Upsilon = -\pi\sqrt{\frac{M}{\xi}} + 2\sqrt{\frac{M}{\xi}}\, \arctan\!\left(\frac{r + a_0 - M}{\sqrt{M\xi}}\right), \quad \xi = 2a_0 - M + 4M_{\rm BH}. $$

The matter density resulting from this mass function is

$$ 4\pi\rho = \frac{m'}{r^2} = \frac{2M\!\left(a_0 + 2M_{\rm BH}\right)\!\left(1-2M_{\rm BH}/r\right)} {r\!\left(r+a_0\right)^3}. $$

The spacetime contains a horizon at $r = 2M_{\rm BH}$ and a curvature singularity at $r = 0$. When the solution parameters satisfy $M_{\rm BH} \ll M \ll a_0$, no further curvature singularities arise. Under this ordering the solution reduces to the Schwarzschild black hole with mass $M_{\rm BH}$ at small radii and to a Hernquist-type matter distribution at large radii, and can therefore be interpreted as a black hole in gravitational interaction with a galactic dark matter halo. The ADM mass of the full configuration is $M_{\rm ADM} = M + M_{\rm BH}$.

Geodesic Structure and the Compactness Parameter

The dark matter halo modifies the photon sphere radius $r_{\rm ph}$ and the innermost stable circular orbit $r_{\rm ISCO}$ relative to their Schwarzschild values. Approximate expressions at first order in $1/a_0$ read

$$ r_{\rm ph} \approx 3M_{\rm BH}\!\left(1 + \frac{M M_{\rm BH}}{a_0^2}\right), \qquad r_{\rm ISCO} \approx 6M_{\rm BH}\!\left(1 - \frac{32 M M_{\rm BH}}{a_0^2}\right). $$

The critical impact parameter on null geodesics, which governs the size of the black hole shadow, is correspondingly shifted to

$$ b_{\rm crit} = 3\sqrt{3}\,M_{\rm BH} \left(1 + \frac{M}{a_0} + \frac{M\!\left(5M - 18M_{\rm BH}\right)}{6a_0^2}\right). $$

Although the dark matter halo is characterised by two independent parameters, $M$ and $a_0$, much of its influence on strong-gravity phenomenology is governed by their ratio — the halo compactness

$$ C = \frac{M}{a_0}. $$

For supermassive black holes at the centres of galaxies, astrophysical constraints on the dark matter content imply compactness values in the range $10^{-8} < C < 10^{-3}$, which defines the parameter space explored in our simulations.
