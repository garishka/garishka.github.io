Linear Polarization for Black Holes with a Dark Matter Halo

September 2024 · T. Angelov, R. Bekir, G. Gyulchev, P. Nedkova, S. Yazadjiev

Scope and Methodology

This section presents a systematic analysis of the linear polarization of synchrotron emission in the static spacetime of a black hole surrounded by a dark matter halo. The central objective is to quantify how the dark matter content modifies the observable polarization pattern relative to the Schwarzschild case, across different magnetic field configurations and inclination angles. We examine both the intensity $I$ and orientation (EVPA) of the polarization vector, with particular emphasis on the role of the halo compactness parameter $C = M/a_0$.

We focus separately on direct images, formed by photon trajectories that reach the observer without completing full loops around the compact object ($\phi \in [0,\pi)$), and on indirect images of order $k = 1$, which involve one additional half-orbit. The deviations in polarization intensity and direction are quantified through

$$ \Delta I = I_{\rm DM} - I_{\rm Sch}, \qquad \Delta\mathrm{EVPA} = \mathrm{EVPA}_{\rm DM} - \mathrm{EVPA}_{\rm Sch}, $$

evaluated point-by-point across the image as a function of the azimuthal coordinate $\phi$. To ensure a meaningful comparison, the emission sources in the two spacetimes are placed at radii that produce the same apparent image size, rather than at identical coordinate radii.

Direct Images

For an inclination angle $i = 20^\circ$ and a purely equatorial magnetic field — the configuration that best reproduces the polarization observations of M87* — simulations show that even in the presence of a dark matter halo the polarization vector maintains its characteristic rotation angle, and the intensity distribution closely follows the Schwarzschild profile. The intensity peaks on the right side of the image and decreases gradually toward the poles, a behavior dominated by gravitational redshift effects at the image edges.

As the halo compactness $M/a_0$ increases, the total polarized flux decreases, but the image symmetry and the positions of all peaks and minima remain in one-to-one correspondence with the Schwarzschild case. Since the dark matter solution reduces to the Schwarzschild geometry in the limit $C \to 0$, the polarization properties become indistinguishably close to those of the isolated black hole at low compactness. The maximum relative deviations for a representative equatorial field configuration $\vec{B} = [0.87, 0.5, 0]$ at $i = 20^\circ$ are

$$ \frac{\max\Delta I}{I_{\rm Sch}} \lesssim 75 \times 10^{-4}, \qquad \frac{\max\Delta\mathrm{EVPA}}{\mathrm{EVPA}_{\rm Sch}} \lesssim 28 \times 10^{-5}, $$

for the largest compactness value $M/a_0 = 10^{-3}$, and both deviations scale linearly with $M/a_0$ across the full range studied. At a higher inclination of $i = 70^\circ$ the numerical values are comparable, confirming that direct images carry less than $1\%$ deviation for all physical compactness values.

The vertical magnetic field configuration $\vec{B} = [0,0,1]$ produces a qualitatively different polarization pattern. It is well established that vertical fields in the Schwarzschild geometry lead to polarization structures inconsistent with M87* observations, owing to the combined effects of aberration and gravitational lensing. Simulations with the dark matter halo confirm that while the overall image structure and symmetry remain similar to the Schwarzschild case, the resulting polarization is again incompatible with astrophysical sources such as M87*. As the halo compactness grows the polarization signal weakens significantly, though the basic morphology — maximum intensity at the bottom of the ring with a gradual decrease upward — is preserved.

Indirect Images

For indirect images of order $k = 1$ the deviations from the Schwarzschild geometry increase by approximately one order of magnitude relative to the direct images, yet remain below $10\%$ for the small inclination angles ($i \lesssim 20^\circ$) relevant to the galactic targets M87* and Sgr A*. At $i = 20^\circ$, for example, a vertical magnetic field yields a maximum EVPA deviation of approximately $4.3\%$ above the Schwarzschild value at compactness $M/a_0 = 10^{-3}$.

A notable structural change appears in the indirect images under a vertical magnetic field: the frequency of variation of polarization peak values along a full orbit doubles, with two characteristic maxima on the left and right sides of the image and two minima at the top and bottom. This feature distinguishes indirect images from direct ones and suggests that higher-order images formed in vertically aligned magnetic fields may ultimately be used to separate black holes from other compact objects in future high-resolution observations.

At the larger inclination of $i = 70^\circ$ the deviations grow further. The maximum enhancement in polarized flux due to the dark matter halo reaches approximately $7.1\%$ above the Schwarzschild value for the configuration $\vec{B} = [0.5, 0.87, 0]$ at $M/a_0 = 10^{-3}$, while for the configuration with the strongest radial component $\vec{B} = [0.87, 0.5, 0]$ this enhancement is reduced by about $34\%$.

Across all configurations and inclinations examined, the overall conclusion is consistent: at astrophysically realistic values of halo compactness the polarization signatures of a black hole surrounded by a dark matter halo are almost indistinguishable from those of an isolated Schwarzschild black hole in direct images, while indirect images provide somewhat better — though still limited — sensitivity to the dark matter content. The detection of the dark matter impact through polarimetric observations of the accretion disk therefore remains extremely challenging with near-future instrumentation.

Role of Halo Compactness

An exception to the general trend of small deviations arises at high compactness, where the increased dark matter content in the halo produces image sizes noticeably larger than those typical of the Schwarzschild black hole. In this regime the light-bending and focusing effects differ more substantially between the two spacetimes, and the comparison at equal apparent image size — rather than equal emission radius — becomes essential. The deviations in both $\Delta I$ and $\Delta\mathrm{EVPA}$ scale linearly with the compactness parameter $C = M/a_0$, so that reducing the compactness by one decade reduces the deviation by the same factor in all magnetic field configurations tested.
