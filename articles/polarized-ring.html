Polarized Image of an Equatorial Emitting Ring

July 2024 · T. Angelov, R. Bekir, G. Gyulchev, P. Nedkova, S. Yazadjiev

Setup and Local Frame

The goal of this analysis is to describe the light reaching a distant observer from a thin fluid ring orbiting at a fixed radial coordinate in the vicinity of a black hole with a dark matter halo. The fluid emits linearly polarized synchrotron radiation in a locally constant magnetic field, and the observable polarization at large distances is obtained by parallel-transporting the polarization four-vector along null geodesics.

To carry out this procedure in the static spherically symmetric spacetime described previously, we introduce an orthonormal tetrad $\{e^\mu_{(a)}\}$ adapted to the metric:

$$ e_{(t)} = \left(1 - \frac{2M_{\rm BH}}{r}\right)^{-1/2} e^{-\Upsilon/2}, \quad e_{(r)} = \left(1 - \frac{2m(r)}{r}\right)^{1/2}\partial_r, \quad e_{(\theta)} = \frac{1}{r}, \quad e_{(\phi)} = \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}. $$

The orbiting fluid moves only in the equatorial $(\hat{r})$–$(\hat{\phi})$ plane. Its velocity in the local frame has magnitude $\beta$ and direction angle $\chi$, so in vector notation

$$ \vec{\beta} = \beta\!\left(\cos\chi\,\hat{r} + \sin\chi\,\hat{\phi}\right). $$

Lorentz Boost and Synchrotron Emission

Since synchrotron emission is most naturally described in the rest frame of the fluid, we switch between frames using the Lorentz transformation $\hat{e}^{\,\mu}_{(a)} = \Lambda^{(b)}{}_{(a)}\,e^\mu_{(b)}$, with the boost matrix

$$ \Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} \gamma & -\beta\gamma\cos\chi & -\beta\gamma\sin\chi & 0 \\ -\beta\gamma\cos\chi & (\gamma-1)\cos^2\!\chi+1 & (\gamma-1)\sin\chi\cos\chi & 0 \\ -\beta\gamma\sin\chi & (\gamma-1)\sin\chi\cos\chi & (\gamma-1)\sin^2\!\chi+1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \gamma = (1-\beta^2)^{-1/2}. $$

The magnetic field permeating the fluid is $\vec{B} = (\hat{B}^r, \hat{B}^\phi, \hat{B}^\theta)$. The polarization direction of the synchrotron emission is determined by the cross product of the local photon three-momentum $\vec{p} = (\hat{p}^r, \hat{p}^\phi, \hat{p}^\theta)$ with the magnetic field:

$$ \vec{f} = \frac{\vec{p} \times \vec{B}}{|\vec{p}|}. $$

The angle $\zeta$ between the magnetic field and the photon three-momentum controls the emission intensity through

$$ \sin\zeta = \frac{|\vec{p}\times\vec{B}|}{|\vec{p}||\vec{B}|}, \qquad \hat{f}^a\hat{f}_a = \sin^2\!\zeta\,|\vec{B}|^2. $$

The polarization four-vector $f^\mu$ is then transformed from the fluid rest frame to the black hole rest frame using the same Lorentz boost $\Lambda$.

Parallel Transport and Constants of Motion

To propagate the polarization to the observer, $f^\mu$ must be parallel-transported along the null geodesic. Since $f^\mu$ remains orthogonal to the photon four-momentum $p^\mu$, both vectors satisfy

$$ p^\mu\nabla_\mu p_\nu = 0, \qquad p^\mu\nabla_\mu f_\nu = 0, \qquad p^\mu f_\mu = 0. $$

In a static spherically symmetric spacetime these differential equations admit an algebraic solution through the hidden symmetries encoded in the Killing–Yano tensor $Y_{\mu\nu}$ and the conformal Killing–Yano tensor $\tilde{Y}_{\mu\nu} = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}Y^{\alpha\beta}$. The non-vanishing components of $Y_{\mu\nu}$ in the black hole with a dark matter halo spacetime are

$$ Y_{\theta\phi} = -Y_{\phi\theta} = r^3\sin\theta. $$

The two associated constants of motion along null geodesics are $\kappa_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{Y}_{\mu\nu}p^\mu f^\nu$ and $\kappa_2 = Y_{\mu\nu}p^\mu f^\nu$. In the dark matter halo spacetime they evaluate to

$$ \kappa_1 = \left(\frac{1-2M_{\rm BH}/r}{1-2m(r)/r}\,e^{\Upsilon}\right)^{1/2} r\!\left(p^t f^r - p^r f^t\right), $$ $$ \kappa_2 = r^3\sin\theta\!\left(p^\theta f^\phi - p^\phi f^\theta\right). $$

Observer Coordinates and Observable Polarization

The celestial coordinates on the observer's sky are defined using the projection of the photon four-momentum onto the local tetrad frame via the angles $\sin\tilde\alpha = p^{(\theta)}/p^{(t)}$ and $\tan\tilde\beta = p^{(\phi)}/p^{(r)}$. After rescaling to $x = r\tilde\alpha$, $y = r\tilde\beta$, the image-plane coordinates become

$$ x = -\frac{p_\phi}{\sin\theta_0}, \qquad y = p_\theta, $$

where $\theta_0$ is the observer's inclination angle. The components of the polarization vector projected onto the observer's frame are

$$ f^x_{\rm obs} = \frac{x\kappa_1 + y\kappa_2}{\sqrt{(\kappa_1^2+\kappa_2^2)(x^2+y^2)}}, \qquad f^y_{\rm obs} = \frac{y\kappa_1 - x\kappa_2}{\sqrt{(\kappa_1^2+\kappa_2^2)(x^2+y^2)}}. $$

These are expressed entirely through the conserved quantities $\kappa_1$ and $\kappa_2$, which can themselves be traced back to the emission point by projecting the polarization vector (defined via the cross-product formula) onto the local tetrad. This yields

$$ \kappa_1 = \gamma r\!\left[\hat{p}^{(t)}\hat{f}^{(r)} + \beta\!\left(\hat{p}^{(r)}\hat{f}^{(\theta)} - \hat{f}^{(r)}\hat{p}^{(\theta)}\right)\right], $$ $$ \kappa_2 = \gamma r\!\left[\left(\hat{p}^{(\theta)}\hat{f}^{(\phi)} - \hat{f}^{(\theta)}\hat{p}^{(\phi)}\right) - \beta\,\hat{p}^{(t)}\hat{f}^{(\phi)}\right]. $$

Polarization Intensity and EVPA

The polarization intensity receives contributions from the angle $\zeta$, the photon frequency, and the geodesic path length through the fluid

$$ l_p = \frac{\hat{p}^{(t)}}{\hat{p}^{(\theta)}}\,H, $$

where $H$ is the disk thickness. The Doppler boost factor between the observer frame and the fluid frame is $\delta = E_{\rm obs}/E_s = 1/\hat{p}^{(t)}$, which modulates the intensity by a factor $\delta^{3+\alpha_\nu}$. The resulting polarization intensity is

$$ |I| = \delta^{3+\alpha_\nu}\,l_p\,(\sin\zeta)^{1+\alpha_\nu}, $$

where the spectral index $\alpha_\nu = 1$ is chosen for consistency with M87* observations. The components of the observed electric field are therefore

$$ E^x_{\rm obs} = \delta^2\,l_p^{1/2}\,\sin\zeta\;f^x_{\rm obs}, \qquad E^y_{\rm obs} = \delta^2\,l_p^{1/2}\,\sin\zeta\;f^y_{\rm obs}, \qquad |I| = (E^x_{\rm obs})^2 + (E^y_{\rm obs})^2. $$

The direction of the polarization vector on the observer's sky is characterised by the electric vector position angle (EVPA), measured counter-clockwise from the positive $x$-semi-axis:

$$ \mathrm{EVPA} = \arctan\!\left(-\frac{f^x_{\rm obs}}{f^y_{\rm obs}}\right). $$
