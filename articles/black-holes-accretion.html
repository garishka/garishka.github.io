Black Holes and Accretion Disk Physics

March 2025 · G. Gyulchev, L. Collodel, S. Yazadjiev, D. Doneva, P. Nedkova

One of the central observational windows into strong-field gravity is the thermal radiation emitted by matter accreting onto compact objects. When gas and dust spiral inward under gravity, viscous dissipation heats the material to temperatures at which it radiates across a broad electromagnetic spectrum — most luminously in the X-ray band for stellar-mass black holes and in the optical-to-ultraviolet range for supermassive ones. The detailed structure of this accretion disk encodes a wealth of information about the underlying spacetime geometry.

The standard theoretical framework is the Novikov–Thorne model, which treats the disk as geometrically thin, optically thick, and in local thermodynamic equilibrium. Each annulus radiates as a blackbody at a temperature determined by the local energy dissipation rate. The luminosity profile peaks near the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO), whose location is set entirely by the mass and spin of the central compact object.

Geodesic Structure and the ISCO

For a Kerr black hole with mass M and dimensionless spin parameter ȧ = a/M ∈ [−1, 1], the ISCO radius in Boyer–Lindquist coordinates depends sensitively on the spin. For a Schwarzschild (non-rotating) black hole the ISCO sits at exactly 6M. As spin increases toward the prograde extremal limit, the ISCO contracts to rISCO → M, dramatically increasing the efficiency of accretion energy conversion.

rISCO = 6 GM / c2   (Schwarzschild limit)

Modifications to general relativity — or the presence of additional matter fields such as scalar hair or a surrounding dark matter halo — can shift the ISCO location and alter the disk's luminosity profile in ways that are, in principle, detectable by current and next-generation X-ray observatories such as NICER, eXTP, and Athena.

Observational Signatures of Disk Emission

The total luminosity emitted by a thin disk between the ISCO and some outer radius is found by integrating the local flux F(r) over the disk surface. The flux itself is determined by the energy, angular momentum, and effective potential of circular geodesics in the given spacetime:

F(r) = − (Ω̇ / (4π√ −g )) ⋅ (E − Ω L)−2 ∫rISCOr (E − Ω L) L̇ dr

where Ω is the angular velocity of circular orbits, E and L are the specific energy and angular momentum of a test particle, and g is the metric determinant. Our group has evaluated these integrals for several parameterised families of alternative compact objects and published the resulting spectral energy distributions for comparison with observational data.

Beyond integrated spectra, the image of the disk — including photon-ring features produced by light bending close to the photon sphere — is now directly accessible to very-long-baseline interferometric arrays. The Event Horizon Telescope's images of M87* and Sgr A* have opened an entirely new regime of spacetime tomography that our theoretical models directly address.

Images of Kerr Black Holes with Scalar Hair

A recent focus of the group is the imaging appearance of Kerr black holes surrounded by synchronized scalar hair — a family of solutions that bifurcates from the Kerr family along a one-parameter set of stationary, rotating boson stars. These solutions, discovered by Herdeiro and Radu, satisfy the no-hair theorems' conditions at the horizon but carry additional structure at finite radii that leaves a distinct imprint on the observed disk luminosity profile and photon ring width.

Using ray-tracing codes developed within the group, we have produced synthetic images of thin accretion disks around these hairy black holes for a range of spin and scalar charge parameters. The results, recently published in IOP Publishing (2025), show systematic deviations from the standard Kerr case that are potentially measurable with next-generation VLBI arrays operating at 345 GHz.
