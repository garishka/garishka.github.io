Polarized Equatorial Emission and Hot Spots Around Black Holes with a Dark Matter Halo

September 2025 · T. Angelov, R. Bekir, G. Gyulchev, P. Nedkova, S. Yazadjiev · European Physical Journal C 85, 1075

Abstract

This work investigates the linear polarization of accretion disk emissions around black holes embedded in a galactic dark matter halo. The gravitational interaction between the black hole and the surrounding dark matter is modelled using an exact solution to the Einstein field equations $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu}$ that superposes a Schwarzschild black hole with a Hernquist-type matter distribution — a density profile well established in galactic simulations and consistent with observed rotation curves.

Observable polarization from a magnetized fluid ring orbiting the black hole is simulated across a range of magnetic field configurations whose parameters are physically motivated by the dark matter distribution in real galaxies. For direct images, polarization intensity and direction deviate by less than $1\%$ from the isolated Schwarzschild case across the full set of configurations tested. For indirect (lensed) images the deviation grows by roughly an order of magnitude yet remains below $10\%$ at the small inclination angles relevant to the galactic targets M87* and Sgr A*. The findings indicate that detecting the imprint of a dark matter halo on accretion-disk polarization will remain extremely challenging with near-future instrumentation.

The Spacetime Geometry

The analysis is conducted in a static, spherically symmetric spacetime that combines a central black hole with a dark matter halo situated at galactic scales. The construction follows the philosophy of the Einstein cluster framework: starting from the Hernquist density profile for the dark matter distribution, one derives an anisotropic energy–momentum tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$ that, together with the black hole mass term, constitutes an exact solution of the Einstein equations. The resulting metric retains the Schwarzschild form in the black hole’s immediate vicinity while the halo contribution becomes significant only at distances characteristic of galactic structure.

$$ ds^2 = -f(r)\,dt^2 + f(r)^{-1}\,dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2 $$

The metric function $f(r)$ encodes both the Schwarzschild mass term and the dark matter contribution. At radii much smaller than the halo scale radius $r_s$, the geometry reduces cleanly to Schwarzschild; deviations grow gradually and become appreciable only beyond the astrophysically relevant accretion region. This separation of scales is crucial for assessing the halo’s observational impact: the near-horizon geodesics that determine photon trajectories and polarization transfer are only weakly perturbed by the halo.

Explicitly, the metric function takes the form

$$ f(r) = 1 - \frac{2M}{r} - \frac{2M_{\mathrm{DM}}(r)}{r} $$

where $M$ is the black hole mass and $M_{\mathrm{DM}}(r)$ is the dark matter mass enclosed within radius $r$, obtained by integrating the Hernquist density profile $\rho_{\mathrm{DM}}(r)$ outward from the origin.

$$ \rho_{\mathrm{DM}}(r) = \frac{M_{\mathrm{DM}}}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{r_s}{r\,(r + r_s)^3} $$

Here $M_{\mathrm{DM}}$ is the total dark matter halo mass and $r_s$ is the characteristic halo scale radius. Both parameters are fixed to values compatible with observational constraints on the dark matter distribution in galaxies hosting M87* and Sgr A*, allowing the simulations to remain physically grounded while spanning a realistic parameter space. The enclosed mass follows as

$$ M_{\mathrm{DM}}(r) = M_{\mathrm{DM}} \left(\frac{r}{r + r_s}\right)^{\!2} $$

A key consequence of this construction is that the event horizon location $r_h$ and the innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) radius $r_{\mathrm{ISCO}}$ remain close to their Schwarzschild values $r_h = 2M$ and $r_{\mathrm{ISCO}} = 6M$ for all halo parameters considered. The effective potential governing photon orbits,

$$ V_{\mathrm{eff}}(r) = f(r)\left(\frac{L^2}{r^2} - \frac{E^2}{f(r)}\right) = \frac{L^2 f(r)}{r^2} - E^2 $$

whose shape determines the photon sphere radius $r_{\mathrm{ph}}$ through the simultaneous conditions $V_{\mathrm{eff}}(r_{\mathrm{ph}}) = 0$ and $V_{\mathrm{eff}}'(r_{\mathrm{ph}}) = 0$, is similarly robust against the dark matter perturbation. Here $E$ and $L$ denote the conserved energy and angular momentum of the photon along its null geodesic, respectively.

This insensitivity at small radii ultimately explains why the polarization signatures in the direct images are so weakly affected by the halo, and motivates the more careful analysis of indirect images, where photons travel on longer paths through the perturbed exterior geometry — accumulating an additional rotation of the polarization angle $\chi$ via Faraday rotation before reaching the observer.

Polarized Emission and Transfer Formalism

The polarized radiative transfer is formulated in terms of the Stokes parameters $\{I, Q, U, V\}$. For synchrotron emission from a magnetized fluid element in the equatorial plane, the two key observables that encode the polarization state of a received photon are the polarization fraction $\Pi$ and the electric vector position angle (EVPA) $\chi$, defined by

$$ \Pi = \frac{\sqrt{Q^2 + U^2}}{I}, \qquad \chi = \frac{1}{2}\arctan\!\left(\frac{U}{Q}\right) $$

The transfer equations along each null geodesic are integrated from the emitting fluid element to the distant observer’s image plane using a parallel-transport frame anchored to the photon four-momentum $k^\mu$. The polarization vector $f^\mu$ is Lie-transported along the geodesic subject to $f^\mu k_\mu = 0$, ensuring the polarization state evolves only due to geometric and plasma effects and not from coordinate artifacts.
